Multiple independent sources document that reliance on generative AI can reduce aspects of cognition.  An MIT Media Lab experiment reported that participants who wrote essays with ChatGPT showed the lowest neural engagement (EEG) and produced lower‑quality, “soulless” essays compared with peers using Google Search or no assistance, indicating diminished critical‑thinking effort【2026/ai-chatgpt-google-learning-school】.  A review by Institut Polytechnique de Paris summarized research showing that AI‑assisted writing cuts cognitive load by about 32 % while speeding output, suggesting reduced memory and attention engagement【2025/generative-ai-the-risk-of-cognitive-atrophy】.  A Microsoft Research survey of 319 knowledge workers found that higher confidence in generative AI correlates with less critical thinking, further linking AI use to lowered cognitive effort【2025/the-impact-of-generative-ai-on-critical-thinking-self-reported-reductions-in-cognitive-effort-and-confidence-effects-from-a-survey-of-knowledge-workers】.  An upcoming randomized controlled trial will measure physiological markers of cognitive effort (eye‑tracking, fNIRS) while participants write with or without ChatGPT, underscoring scholarly interest in quantifying this effect【2025/pmc12255134】.  The evidence focuses primarily on student and knowledge‑worker populations, with limited data on other user groups.  Google Cloud’s responsible‑AI documentation acknowledges broader AI risks and offers tooling and guidelines to mitigate potential harms, but does not refute the cognitive‑effort findings【2026/responsible-ai】【2026/principles】.
