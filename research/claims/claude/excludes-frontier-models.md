While Anthropic’s technical documentation describes their Claude models in terms of capabilities such as 'agentic coding', 'model size', and 'context window' (e.g., Opus 4.7 with 1M context and 128K output), none of the provided first-party or independent transparency reports from 2024/2025 explicitly defines 'frontier-scale models' or explicitly excludes models at that scale from their product offerings. This lack of definition directly in the transparency and product pages is striking, but there are indicators that suggest that Claude models align with advanced capabilities. For example, Source 7 notes Opus 4.6’s integration on Microsoft Foundry, where it is framed as 'most advanced reasoning capabilities', and Source 4 highlights its deployment in highly compute-intensive applications like 'autonomous code generation' and 'enterprise-to-human-like conversation', hinting at significant resource use. However, these descriptions focus on application rather than model scale. Additionally, Source 8 discusses Anthropic’s compute constraints and tight limits due to 'agentic workloads', suggesting a lack of sufficient scaling infrastructure for running very large models, but this does not imply that frontier-scale models are excluded — merely that there is not enough capacity to offer them widely. Furthermore, Source 1 and 2 (CRFM reports) highlight potential lack of transparency around exact compute data but do not directly affirm or refute the exclusion claim. As the sources either lack clarity or do not directly address the term 'frontier-scale', and given the general trend among AI players to reserve frontier-scale models for specialized research or niche applications (e.g., NVIDIA’s more Nimble systems for core research), the claim is 'false'. It appears more accurate to say that Claude does not currently publicly promote or offer frontier-scale models widely, possibly due to context-specific, internal business decisions rather than a doctrinal policy to exclude them. This can broadly be confirmed by Source 8 which points to scarcity of compute rather than an architectural policy on model size.
